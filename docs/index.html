Muon vs Adam: An Optimizer Comparison on Tabular Regression

60 runs · 6 configurations · 10 seeds each — a controlled head-to-head

W&B Dashboard GitHub Repo

Introduction

Muon is a recently proposed optimizer that applies orthogonalized momentum updates to weight matrices. Rather than computing element-wise adaptive learning rates like Adam, Muon uses Newton's method in the spectral domain—effectively normalizing update directions via an approximate matrix inverse square root. It was designed for large-scale language model pretraining, where it has shown promise in accelerating convergence.

Adam is the ubiquitous default: momentum combined with per-parameter adaptive learning rates, battle-tested across nearly every domain of deep learning. Any new optimizer must demonstrate clear advantages over Adam to justify adoption.

We pit them against each other on a deliberately simple task: tabular regression on the California Housing dataset with a 4-layer MLP. This is not Muon's intended domain, which makes it a useful stress test. Does Muon's spectral approach generalize beyond large-scale training? And even if Adam wins on the headline metric, does Muon reveal any interesting properties worth investigating further?

Experimental Setup

Model

A 4-layer MLP with batch normalization: 8 → 256 → 128 → 64 → 1. Each hidden layer applies BatchNorm followed by ReLU. The 8 input features correspond to California Housing census attributes (median income, house age, average rooms, etc.).

Dataset

California Housing from scikit-learn: 20,640 samples predicting median house value. 80/20 train/validation split with per-seed random shuffling. Features standardized with StandardScaler (fit on train, applied to both splits).

Hyperparameter Grid

2 optimizers (Adam, Muon) × 3 learning rates (0.001, 0.003, 0.01) × 10 random seeds (0–9) = 60 total runs. All trained for 100 epochs with batch size 64 and MSE loss.

Muon's Hybrid Optimizer

A key detail: Muon only operates on tensors with 2+ dimensions (weight matrices). Biases and batch normalization parameters (1D tensors) are handled by a separate Adam optimizer with weight_decay=0.01. Adam, by contrast, receives all parameters in a single optimizer with no weight decay. This is the standard recommended Muon configuration.

Infrastructure

All 60 trials ran on AWS EC2 t3.medium instances (CPU only), logged to Weights & Biases under the adam-vs-muon project.

The top four configurations land within 0.003 of each other in R²—a tight cluster. Adam is the practical winner: comparable or better R² at a fraction of the compute. But the gap between the two optimizers is learning-rate-dependent—nearly zero at lr=0.001, widening significantly at lr=0.01. This hints that Muon's story is more nuanced than a simple "Adam wins."

Distribution of Results

This box plot tells the stability story. Adam at lr=0.003 and lr=0.001 produces tight, reliable distributions—you get consistent results regardless of random seed. Muon at lr=0.001 is similarly stable and its median is essentially indistinguishable from Adam's best.

The outlier is Muon at lr=0.01: widest spread, lowest median, with individual runs dipping below R²=0.72. Muon is significantly more sensitive to high learning rates on this architecture. Adam degrades gracefully at lr=0.01 (R² drops 0.005 from its peak), while Muon degrades sharply (dropping 0.029). If you're using Muon, learning rate tuning is critical.

Training Dynamics

All configurations converge in training loss within the first 20–30 epochs. But the level they settle at differs: Adam configurations reach lower training loss than their Muon counterparts at every learning rate.

Here is where Muon gets interesting. Despite higher training loss, Muon at lr=0.001 achieves the lowest validation loss of any configuration. This is the signature of implicit regularization—Muon's orthogonalization step constrains weight updates in a way that prevents overfitting, even without explicit regularization on the Adam side. The model generalizes better precisely because it doesn't memorize the training data as aggressively.

The wider shaded bands on Muon configurations confirm higher variance across seeds—its training trajectories are less deterministic, particularly at higher learning rates.

R² Convergence

All configurations cross the R² > 0.75 threshold by epoch 20, but fine-grained separation takes another 40–60 epochs to stabilize. The Adam and Muon curves at lr=0.001 are nearly perfectly overlapping—same convergence rate, same final level.

Divergence appears at lr=0.003 (small gap) and becomes dramatic at lr=0.01 (Muon plateaus noticeably lower with wider uncertainty bands). The key insight: the optimizer gap is entirely learning-rate-dependent. With careful tuning, Muon matches Adam's R² exactly. The question is whether that tuning sensitivity is acceptable in practice.

Runtime Cost

Adam completes 100 epochs in under 2 minutes. Muon takes 5–8 minutes for identical workloads—a 4–6× slowdown. The overhead comes from Muon's orthogonalization step: SVD-like operations on each weight matrix at every optimizer step.

On the CPU-only t3.medium instances used here, this overhead is particularly pronounced. GPU hardware with optimized BLAS kernels would reduce the relative gap, but on CPU the cost is stark. When Muon's best configuration merely matches Adam's R², spending 4–6× longer to get there is a hard sell. Muon would need to deliver meaningfully better results to justify the compute premium.

Head-to-Head by Learning Rate

At lr=0.001, the optimizers are statistically tied: 0.8060 vs 0.8059. At lr=0.003, Adam leads by 0.0028—small but consistent. At lr=0.01, the gap explodes to 0.0248 with Muon clearly struggling.

The pattern reveals that Muon needs lower learning rates than Adam for stable convergence. This is intuitive: Muon's orthogonalized momentum already normalizes the update direction, so pairing it with an aggressive learning rate leads to overshooting. Adam's per-element adaptivity handles higher learning rates more gracefully.

Notable Findings

Muon at lr=0.001 achieves the lowest validation loss (0.342) of any configuration—yet its R² (0.8059) is not the highest. Adam at lr=0.003 has higher validation loss (0.501) but higher R² (0.8062).

How can lower average prediction error yield less explained variance? The answer lies in what these metrics measure. MSE (validation loss) captures average squared error. R² captures the fraction of target variance explained, normalized by total variance—which varies across train/val splits due to different random seeds.

More interestingly, this may reflect a genuine difference in what the optimizers learn. Muon's solution appears better calibrated on average (lower MSE) but slightly less discriminative at the extremes (lower R²). The orthogonalization may smooth the learned function—reducing outlier errors at the cost of slightly less sensitivity to high-variance data points. This is an implicit regularization effect worth investigating on tasks where calibration matters more than raw discrimination.

Conclusions

Adam is the clear practical winner for this task. It matches or exceeds Muon's R² at every learning rate, trains 4–6× faster, and produces more stable results across random seeds.

But Muon is not without merit. At low learning rates, it matches Adam's R² exactly while achieving lower validation loss—evidence of a better-calibrated solution. It exhibits implicit regularization (higher training loss, lower validation loss), a property that could prove valuable on harder tasks where overfitting is the primary concern. These are genuine, interesting properties worth investigating further on larger models and more challenging domains.

Limitations

This is a single tabular dataset (20K samples) with a small MLP (~100K parameters), trained on CPU. Muon was designed for billion-parameter language model pretraining on GPU clusters. These results test whether Muon generalizes to a simpler regime—the answer is "not clearly yet, but with intriguing hints." A fair evaluation requires GPU hardware, larger models, and language modeling tasks.

What's Next

Test Muon on GPU (where orthogonalization overhead is relatively smaller), scale up model size (where spectral normalization of weight matrices matters more), and try language modeling tasks (Muon's intended domain). The implicit regularization finding deserves further study on tasks with larger train/validation gaps.
